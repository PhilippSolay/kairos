# Task-Prioritization & Personal-Productivity Frameworks: A Critical Survey

**Audience context.** This survey is written for a systems-thinker / architect / designer running **two companies** with many concurrent projects. The defining failure mode is **OVERWHELM → FREEZE**: when responsibilities pile up, he avoids the important work and builds easy things as a distraction (classic "productive procrastination"). Every framework below is therefore judged not only on its general merits but on one specific question: **does it reduce the freeze, or does it feed it?**

A note on the failure mode itself, because it changes the verdicts. Task paralysis is a *nervous-system* response, not a discipline problem: when the brain perceives an overwhelming or high-stakes load, it triggers a freeze similar to fight-or-flight, and "productive procrastination" is the avoidance behaviour that follows — doing everything on the list *except* the one thing that matters ([Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/the-science-of-stuck/202301/feeling-stuck-2-types-of-procrastination), [Surviving Life Lessons](https://www.survivinglifelessons.com/post/don-t-procrastinate-overwhelm-stress-and-avoidance)). This has two consequences for framework selection:

1. **Methods that expand the visible decision space make freeze worse.** A 25-goal list, a fully-loaded GTD system, or an open Kanban board with no cap all *increase* the number of things competing for attention. For a freezer, more visible choices = more threat = more avoidance.
2. **Methods that shrink the decision to "one obvious next thing" defuse the freeze.** Constraint is the therapy. The most valuable property a framework can have here is *forced reduction of choice*.

Keep that lens in mind through the verdicts.

## Part 1 — The Methods

### 1. Getting Things Done (GTD)

**What it is.** David Allen's five-stage system: **Capture** everything that has your attention into a trusted external system, **Clarify** each item into a concrete outcome and a single **next action**, **Organize** into lists/calendar/reference, **Reflect** via a weekly review across six "horizons," and **Engage** by trusting the system to tell you what to do now. The core philosophy is to get everything *except the current task* out of your head, eliminating the background anxiety of unremembered commitments ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Getting_Things_Done), [gettingthingsdone.com](https://gettingthingsdone.com/what-is-gtd/), [Todoist](https://www.todoist.com/productivity-methods/getting-things-done)).

**When it shines.** High-volume, multi-project knowledge work — exactly this person's situation. The "next action" concept is the single best anti-freeze primitive in productivity literature: it converts a vague, scary project ("relaunch Company B website") into a small, non-threatening physical act ("draft the homepage hero copy"). Capture also frees working memory, which directly reduces the cognitive load that triggers the freeze.

**When it fails.** GTD is famously *heavy*. The full system has many moving parts, and the weekly review — which Allen calls the "critical factor for success" — is the part people abandon first. Crucially for a freezer: **GTD captures and organizes but does not prioritize.** It produces large context-sorted lists ("@computer," "@calls") and leaves you to decide, in the moment, what matters most. Staring at a 200-item Next Actions list is itself a freeze trigger. GTD assumes the bottleneck is *memory and organization*; for this user the bottleneck is *choice under threat*.

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: Medium.** The capture + next-action discipline is genuinely therapeutic and should be adopted. But adopted *whole*, GTD's sprawling lists can amplify the freeze. Take the two primitives (capture, next-action), pair them with a hard prioritization/limiting layer, and skip the maximalist version.

### 2. Eisenhower Matrix (Urgent / Important)

**What it is.** A 2×2 grid sorting tasks by **Urgent vs. Not Urgent** and **Important vs. Not Important**, yielding four actions: **Do** (urgent+important), **Decide/Schedule** (important, not urgent — the quadrant Covey called "Quality"), **Delegate** (urgent, not important), and **Delete** (neither) ([Asana](https://asana.com/resources/eisenhower-matrix), [Todoist](https://www.todoist.com/productivity-methods/eisenhower-matrix)).

**When it shines.** It is the clearest teaching device for the single most important distinction this person needs: **urgent ≠ important.** Freezers and overwhelmed founders systematically over-serve Quadrant 3 (other people's urgencies) and the "easy build" trap lives in Quadrant 4 disguised as Q1. The matrix names the trap. Quadrant 2 — important-but-not-urgent — is precisely the strategic work he avoids.

**When it fails.** In the real world "the distinction between urgent/non-urgent and important/not important is much murkier than under experimental conditions" ([Todoist](https://www.todoist.com/productivity-methods/eisenhower-matrix)). Most real tasks are 60/40, not clean corners, and the binary axes hide the *degree* of importance and the *deadline distance*. It is a sorting heuristic, not an ordering algorithm — it tells you the quadrant, not which of your nine Q2 items to start. Living in Q1 also burns you out.

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: Medium.** Excellent as a *conceptual filter* and a weekly triage lens (it's how you flag avoidance), weak as a daily "do this next" engine because it doesn't produce an ordered list. Use it to classify importance/urgency as *inputs* to a finer scoring model rather than as the final decision.

### 3. Most Important Tasks (MITs) / The Ivy Lee Method

**What it is.** At the end of each day, write down the **six** most important things for tomorrow, **rank them in order of importance**, and the next day work them strictly in sequence — finish #1 before touching #2. Unfinished items roll to the next day's list. MITs is the looser modern cousin (typically 1–3 must-do tasks per day) ([James Clear](https://jamesclear.com/ivy-lee), [Todoist](https://www.todoist.com/productivity-methods/ivy-lee-method)).

**When it shines.** This is arguably the **best single anti-freeze method on the list**, for three structural reasons. (1) **The cap of six forces realistic planning and reduces overwhelm** by construction — you physically cannot list 40 things. (2) **Deciding the night before removes morning deliberation**, so you wake into action instead of into choice-paralysis ([James Clear](https://jamesclear.com/ivy-lee)). (3) **Strict sequential execution defeats the avoidance shuffle** — you're not allowed to skip #1 (the scary important thing) to go build the easy thing at #4. Its simplicity *is* the feature: "complexity makes it harder to get back on track."

**When it fails.** It is oversimplified: no native handling of emergencies, dependencies, or appointments, and it assumes you ranked correctly. Six can still be too many on a heavy day. And it has no *cross-day, cross-company* view — it's a daily tool, blind to the portfolio.

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: High.** The daily shortlist + ordered execution is the exact antidote to "too many visible choices." Its only gap (it doesn't know your overall priorities) is filled by pairing it with a scoring/triage layer that *feeds* the six.

### 4. Eat That Frog (Brian Tracy)

**What it is.** Identify your single most important task — your "frog," defined by **impact relative to your goals**, not by how much you dread it — and **do it first thing in the morning**, before email or reactive work, while willpower and focus are highest ([Todoist](https://www.todoist.com/productivity-methods/eat-the-frog), [Zapier](https://zapier.com/blog/eat-that-frog/)).

**When it shines.** Directly targets the avoidance pattern. The whole point is to spend your best cognitive hours on the high-impact thing *before* the day's easy distractions can capture you. For someone who builds easy things to avoid hard ones, "frog first, before email" is a behavioural circuit-breaker.

**When it fails.** The critical flaw: **it only works if you're eating the *right* frog.** "When your task list has no connection to your goals, deciding your biggest task each morning becomes guesswork… you end up disciplined and busy at the same time — finishing hard things that were never that important" ([Todoist](https://www.todoist.com/productivity-methods/eat-the-frog)). For a two-company operator, *which* frog (Company A's fundraising deck vs. Company B's hiring decision) is the actual hard question — and Eat That Frog assumes that's already answered. It also addresses only one task; it's a daily focusing ritual, not a prioritization system.

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: Medium-High.** The "frog-first" *behaviour* is exactly right and should be a daily rule. But it must be bolted onto a method that reliably *picks* the right frog, or it risks energizing avoidance-disguised-as-productivity.

### 5. ABCDE Method (Brian Tracy)

**What it is.** Take your full task list and label each: **A** = must do, serious consequences if not (sub-rank A-1, A-2…); **B** = should do, mild consequences; **C** = nice to do, no consequences; **D** = delegate; **E** = eliminate. Then start immediately on A-1 and stay on it until done ([Brian Tracy](https://www.briantracy.com/blog/time-management/the-abcde-list-technique-for-setting-priorities/)).

**When it shines.** It adds the *consequence* dimension that the Eisenhower Matrix glosses over ("what actually happens if I don't?") and it builds in **delegate** and **eliminate** as first-class moves — vital for someone running two companies who must offload and kill work, not just sequence it. The A-1 instruction produces the same single-focus anchor as Eat That Frog.

**When it fails.** Like MoSCoW, it's a coarse bucketing scheme with subjective boundaries (your B vs. C is a judgement call) and no math, deadline-awareness, or effort-awareness. With many tasks you can end up with fifteen "A" items — which reproduces the overwhelm it was meant to cure unless you ruthlessly sub-rank.

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: Medium.** The "consequence" framing and the delegate/eliminate actions are valuable, and A-1 gives focus. But on its own it doesn't constrain enough — the discipline lives entirely in honest labelling, which a stressed brain games. Best used as the *importance* input to a scoring model.

### 6. Pareto / 80-20 Prioritization

**What it is.** The observation that ~80% of results come from ~20% of inputs ([Asana](https://asana.com/resources/pareto-principle-80-20-rule), [Hive](https://hive.com/blog/pareto-principle/)). Applied to tasks: find the vital few activities driving most of the value and concentrate there.

**When it shines.** As a *strategic lens* it is powerful for a systems-thinker: across two companies, a handful of decisions/projects drive most outcomes, and Pareto gives explicit permission to deprioritize the long tail. It pairs naturally with Buffett's 5/25 (below) as the "what's even worth tracking" filter.

**When it fails.** It's a heuristic, not a procedure — it doesn't tell you *which* 20% (that's the whole problem), and the ratio is illustrative, not literal. It also has a known trap: people over-focus on high-value work and let small-but-mandatory items (compliance, correspondence, dependencies) slip through the cracks ([PM Study Circle](https://pmstudycircle.com/80-20-rule/)). Some low-value tasks are non-negotiable.

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: Medium.** Excellent at the *weekly/quarterly* altitude (it justifies saying no and shrinking the portfolio), largely useless at the *daily* "do this next" altitude. It informs importance scoring but can't operate it.

### 7. Time-Blocking & Timeboxing (incl. Cal Newport Deep Work, Day Theming)

**What it is.** Divide the day (or week) into blocks each dedicated to a specific task or batch. Newport's *Deep Work* variant schedules every minute, protecting long uninterrupted blocks for cognitively demanding work and batching shallow tasks (email) into tight windows. **Day theming** assigns whole days to a domain (e.g., "Mondays = Company A, Tuesdays = Company B"). Time-blockers report roughly **2× the work per week** versus reactive methods, plus a cleaner work/non-work boundary and less stress; Newport stresses the plan is meant to be *edited* through the day, not obeyed rigidly ([Todoist](https://www.todoist.com/productivity-methods/time-blocking), [Make Headway](https://makeheadway.com/blog/cal-newport-time-blocking/), [Cal Newport](https://calnewport.com/the-time-blocking-revolution-begins/)).

**When it shines.** Time-blocking is the **execution layer** that the priority methods lack — a prioritized list tells you *what*, time-blocking commits *when*. For deep, important, avoided work, a protected block ("9–11am: Company A board memo, phone in drawer") makes starting the default. **Day theming is especially apt for the two-company problem**: it ends the exhausting all-day context-switching between businesses by giving each a container.

**When it fails.** Reactive, interruption-heavy days shred a rigid schedule, and a blown plan can itself trigger guilt-freeze ("I'm already behind, why bother"). Estimating block length is hard; founders systematically under-budget. It schedules time but doesn't *decide importance* — you can perfectly time-block the wrong work. It also pairs poorly with a brittle perfectionist mindset unless treated as a flexible guide.

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: Medium-High.** As the *commitment device* for the day's top 1–3 items it's superb — protecting a block for the frog is how you actually eat it. The risk is over-scheduling a fragile day. Use lightly: block the important work and energy peaks, leave the rest fluid.

### 8. Personal Kanban with WIP Limits (Benson & Barry) — *given special weight*

**What it is.** Jim Benson & Tonianne DeMaria Barry's adaptation of Toyota Kanban to individuals, built on **exactly two rules**: (1) **Visualize your work** — every task on a board (Ready / Doing / Done); (2) **Limit WIP** — an explicit cap on how many items may be *in progress at once*. They recommend starting around a **WIP limit of 3** for individuals and tuning from there; too low (1–2) can create idle time when blocked ([Personal Kanban](https://personalkanban.com/glossary/wip-limit-work-in-progress-limit/), [Wrike](https://www.wrike.com/kanban-guide/personal-kanban/), [Kanban Tool](https://kanbantool.com/kanban-library/why-kanban/why-limit-your-wip)).

**Why WIP limits directly counter overwhelm.** This is the one mechanism on the entire list that attacks the *root cause* rather than a symptom. Benson & Barry's own framing: "Limiting work in progress is essential for eliminating the fatigue and overwhelm that comes from having to manage too many tasks at once." Both rules address the same root problem — *too many concurrent commitments* — through different mechanisms. The supporting logic is concrete: starting more and more things "creates distraction, loss of focus and therefore a drop in the overall quality of work"; every unfinished item carries an ongoing *context-switching tax* (the cost of re-loading the mental frame each time you return to it); and "do something badly = do it twice" ([Kanban Tool](https://kanbantool.com/kanban-library/why-kanban/why-limit-your-wip)).

For the freeze specifically: a WIP cap of 2–3 converts "I have 40 open responsibilities" into "I am allowed to be working on at most 3 things, and I cannot pull a new card until one finishes." That **forced reduction of the active set is itself the anti-freeze intervention** — it shrinks the threat surface, and it makes "starting the easy distraction" illegal because the easy thing would have to be one of your 2–3 *chosen* cards. Visualization adds a second benefit: seeing the whole board *outside your head* relieves the working-memory load that drives the panic, while the cap stops the visible board from becoming its own source of dread.

**When it fails.** A board has no built-in notion of *importance* or *deadline* — WIP-limiting the wrong three tasks just makes you efficiently do unimportant work. Pure pull-based flow under-serves hard deadlines. And the visualize-everything rule, taken literally with hundreds of cards in "Ready," can re-create overwhelm — the backlog column needs its own discipline (or a Buffett-style cull).

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: High.** The WIP limit is the strongest structural defence against this user's failure mode and should be a non-negotiable part of the final system. Its blind spot (no importance/deadline sense) is exactly what a scoring layer supplies — so Kanban becomes the *container*, and scoring decides *what gets pulled into it*.

### 9. Weighted Scoring Models: RICE, ICE, WSJF, MoSCoW

These replace gut feel with explicit arithmetic — appealing to a systems-thinker who will trust a model he can inspect and tune.

**RICE.** Score = **(Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort**. Reach = how many affected; Impact = magnitude (3 / 2 / 1 / 0.5 / 0.25); Confidence = % certainty in your estimates; Effort = person-months. Higher = do first ([Product School](https://productschool.com/blog/product-fundamentals/rice-framework), [ProductPlan](https://www.productplan.com/glossary/rice-scoring-model)). *Strength:* the **Confidence** term explicitly discounts shiny-but-speculative work, and **÷ Effort** rewards quick wins — useful momentum-builders for a frozen person. *Weakness:* "Reach" is awkward for personal/internal tasks, and the multiplication makes scores volatile. *Personal-use verdict:* adaptable but needs Reach reinterpreted as "scope/leverage."

**ICE.** A lighter RICE: **Impact × Confidence × Ease** (or Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort). *Strength:* fast, three inputs, low overhead — friendlier to a freezer than RICE. *Weakness:* even more subjective; easy to fudge to justify what you already wanted to do.

**WSJF (Weighted Shortest Job First — SAFe).** **WSJF = Cost of Delay ÷ Job Size**, where **Cost of Delay = Business Value + Time Criticality + Risk Reduction / Opportunity Enablement**, each scored on a modified Fibonacci scale (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20). Highest WSJF goes first; it's rooted in Don Reinertsen's queueing-theory work on cost of delay ([Scaled Agile](https://framework.scaledagile.com/wsjf), [ProductPlan](https://www.productplan.com/glossary/weighted-shortest-job-first)). *Why a systems-thinker will love it:* it makes the **economics of delay** explicit and bakes **deadlines** in as *Time Criticality* — it answers "is the cost of waiting growing?", which neither RICE nor Eisenhower does. Dividing by Job Size means small-high-value work is sequenced first, building momentum. *Weakness:* the relative Fibonacci scoring needs calibration and is heavier than a daily tool should be; "Business Value" can be guessed. *Fit:* its DNA — **value + time-criticality + risk, divided by size** — is the best blueprint for the custom scoring model proposed in Part 3.

**MoSCoW.** Buckets requirements into **Must / Should / Could / Won't (this time)**. *Strength:* the explicit **Won't** category is excellent — it makes *not doing things* a visible, decided act, which a two-company operator needs. *Weakness:* "no objective methodology for ranking initiatives against each other," subjective Should-vs-Could boundary, no dependencies, no ordering within a bucket ([ProductPlan](https://www.productplan.com/glossary/moscow-prioritization), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MoSCoW_method)). It's a coarse triage, not a sequencer.

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE (scoring models overall): Medium — but high *strategic* value.** Scoring is the rational antidote to "guessing the frog": it tells you *which* important thing is actually most important, and a number is reassuringly un-debatable to a stressed mind. The danger is **analysis paralysis** — a freezer can hide *inside* the scoring spreadsheet as a fresh form of productive procrastination. The resolution: run the scoring **weekly, in batch** (not per-task, not daily) to generate the ranked candidate pool, then let the daily tools execute. WSJF's logic is the keeper; RICE/ICE contribute the effort and confidence terms; MoSCoW's "Won't" contributes the cull.

### 10. Warren Buffett 5/25 Rule

**What it is.** Write your top **25** goals; circle the **5** most important; then treat the remaining **20** as an *"avoid at all costs" list* — they get **zero** attention until the top 5 succeed. The power isn't in focusing on 5, it's in *actively refusing* the other 20, which "feel meaningful but are in fact dangerous distractions" ([ModelThinkers](https://modelthinkers.com/mental-model/buffetts-two-lists), [Fellow](https://fellow.ai/blog/the-5-25-rule-how-to-apply-it-example/)).

**When it shines.** This is the **portfolio-level** governor the two-company situation desperately needs. The freezer's deepest problem isn't today's task — it's that *too many things are legitimately on the table at all*. 5/25 is elimination as a discipline, and naming the 20 as "avoid at all costs" reframes the easy-distraction projects (often the appealing builds!) as the enemy of the five. It's the perfect partner to Pareto.

**When it fails.** Brutal and low-resolution: it's about *goals/quarters*, not tasks/days, and "zero attention to 20 things" is unrealistic when some of the 20 are maintenance obligations of a running company. Five can still be too many to *execute* simultaneously (that's what WIP limits fix). It's a strategic carve, not an operating system.

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: High (as the top-of-funnel filter).** Used quarterly to shrink the portfolio *before* anything reaches the daily list, it prevents overwhelm at the source. It doesn't run the day — but it decides what's even allowed to compete for the day.

### 11. Energy Management / Ultradian Rhythms / Task-to-Energy Matching

**What it is.** Manage *energy*, not just time. The body cycles through **ultradian rhythms** of ~90–120 minutes alternating high alertness and a recovery dip (first described by Kleitman). The practice: schedule your hardest, deepest work into your 90-minute peak windows, drop to lighter tasks during the dip, and take real restorative breaks. One cited study reports professionals who aligned work to 90-minute cycles saw ~40% higher productivity and ~50% less mental fatigue ([Asian Efficiency](https://www.asianefficiency.com/productivity/ultradian-rhythms/), [Medium/Morris](https://gm3.medium.com/using-your-natural-cycles-of-ultradian-rhythm-to-boost-performance-2ba20f3afd6)).

**When it shines.** Reframes the freeze as *low-energy mismatch* rather than weakness — a more compassionate and more actionable diagnosis. Matching the avoided important task to a genuine energy peak (rather than attempting it depleted at 4pm, failing, and confirming "I can't") materially raises the odds of starting. It explains *why* "frog first in the morning" works physiologically, and it makes the recovery dip a legitimate place for the easy/admin work — channelling the distraction urge instead of fighting it.

**When it fails.** No prioritization content whatsoever — it's a *scheduling/recovery* lens, and you can perfectly energy-match the wrong task. Individual rhythms vary and meeting-heavy founder days rarely bend to a clean 90-minute cadence. Overstated as a standalone "system."

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: Medium-High (as a modifier, not a system).** It shouldn't *choose* tasks, but it should *place* them: put the scored top task in the peak window. As an input to the scoring model — "does this task's demand match my current energy?" — it meaningfully improves the odds the hard thing actually gets started.

### 12. Weekly & Daily Review Rituals

**What it is.** Two cadences. **Daily planning / shutdown ritual** (~10–15 min): at day's end, review open loops, confirm each is either captured or has a trusted plan, and set tomorrow's shortlist — creating a clean work/home boundary and letting the mind rest. **Weekly review**: step back from the grind, reflect on what went well/badly, surface patterns, and re-align the week to goals ([Cal Newport](https://calnewport.com/drastically-reduce-stress-with-a-work-shutdown-ritual/), [Todoist](https://www.todoist.com/productivity-methods/weekly-review), [FacileThings](https://facilethings.com/blog/en/the-importance-of-shutdown-rituals-in-personal-productivity)).

**When it shines.** Reviews are the **maintenance layer that keeps every other method honest** and the single biggest defence against drift back into overwhelm. For a freezer the benefits are specific: the **shutdown ritual offloads open loops so the mind can rest** (capturing everything "makes recurring thoughts about pending tasks practically disappear"), which directly lowers the chronic anxiety that fuels the freeze; **setting tomorrow's list the night before** means waking into action, not deliberation; and the **weekly review is where avoidance gets caught** — "this important task has rolled over five days running" is only visible from the weekly altitude.

**When it fails.** They are the first thing dropped when busy — precisely when most needed — and an over-engineered review (the maximalist GTD weekly review) becomes a dreaded chore that's skipped, defeating the purpose. They require honesty; a stressed person can perform a review without confronting the avoided item.

**Fit for OVERWHELM → FREEZE: High (as connective tissue).** A lightweight daily shutdown + a short weekly review are non-negotiable scaffolding for this user. Daily review feeds the next-day shortlist; weekly review re-runs the scoring, catches avoidance, and re-applies the WIP/portfolio limits. Keep them *short* or they'll be abandoned.

## Part 3 — Synthesis

### Which 2–3 methods, combined, best fit someone who freezes under overload?

**The hypothesis to pressure-test was:** *tiny daily shortlist + strict WIP limit + lightweight importance/urgency/deadline scoring + weekly review.*

**Verdict: the hypothesis is correct and survives pressure-testing — with two refinements.** Each component maps to a distinct *altitude* of the freeze problem, and the failure of single-method approaches is precisely that they operate at one altitude and leave the others open:

- **The freeze has three altitudes.** (1) *Portfolio* — too many legitimate commitments exist at all. (2) *Active set* — too many things in progress right now. (3) *Moment* — too many visible choices when deciding what to touch. A method that only addresses one altitude leaves the freeze fully armed at the other two. This is why no single framework works for this user.
- **WIP limit (Kanban) is non-negotiable** — it's the only mechanism attacking the *active-set* altitude (the root cause), and it makes the easy-distraction detour structurally illegal because the easy thing would have to be one of your chosen 2–3 cards.
- **The tiny daily shortlist (Ivy Lee / MITs, executed frog-first) is non-negotiable** — it's the only mechanism attacking the *moment* altitude, collapsing the decision to "do #1," made the night before so you wake into action.
- **A lightweight scoring layer is needed** — but it earns its place by *feeding* the shortlist with the right items, not by running daily. Without it, the shortlist and WIP cap will faithfully execute the *wrong* work (the Eat-That-Frog failure mode). Its risk — analysis-paralysis-as-procrastination — is contained by running it **weekly in batch**, never per-task.
- **The weekly review is the connective tissue** that re-runs scoring, catches rollover/avoidance, and re-applies the limits.

**Two refinements to the hypothesis:**

1. **Add an explicit portfolio cull above the scoring layer** — Buffett 5/25 (or Pareto + MoSCoW's "Won't"), run quarterly. The hypothesis starts at the task layer, but for a *two-company* operator the overwhelm originates one level up, in the number of active goals. Cap the portfolio first, or the scoring layer drowns in candidates.
2. **Add energy-matching and a single time-block as the execution refinement** — not as core methods, but as the rule that *places* the day's #1 task into a genuine energy peak in a protected block. This is what converts "I picked the frog" into "I actually ate it."

**The recommended stack (4 layers, by altitude):**

| Layer | Cadence | Method(s) | Job |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Portfolio governor** | Quarterly | **Buffett 5/25** + Pareto + MoSCoW "Won't" | Cap active goals to ~5; everything else is "avoid at all costs" |
| **Priority engine** | Weekly (batch) | **WSJF-style scoring** (+RICE's effort/confidence) | Rank the candidate pool into an ordered queue |
| **Load container** | Continuous | **Personal Kanban, WIP ≤ 3** | Cap concurrent in-progress work; block easy-detour pulls |
| **Daily execution** | Daily | **Ivy Lee top 3, frog-first** + 1 time-block in energy peak | Collapse the day to "do #1 now" |
| *(glue)* | Daily + weekly | **Shutdown + weekly review** | Set tomorrow's list; re-run scoring; catch avoidance |

The two indispensable, *if-you-only-do-two* picks: **WIP-limited Personal Kanban + a frog-first Ivy Lee daily shortlist.** Together they cover the active-set and moment altitudes — the two that produce the actual freeze.

### A Concrete Scoring-Model Proposal: the "Do This Next" score

A single number, computed **weekly in batch** for every candidate task, producing a ranked queue. The daily shortlist is simply the top of this queue, filtered by what the WIP cap allows. Built on **WSJF's spine** (value + time-criticality + risk, divided by size), with deliberate additions for this user's failure mode.

**Inputs (each 1–5 unless noted):**

| Input | Scale | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Importance** (`I`) | 1–5 | Consequence if undone (ABCDE "A" logic): 5 = serious strategic/financial consequence, 1 = nobody notices |
| **Deadline proximity** (`D`) | 1–5 | 5 = due ≤2 days / cost-of-delay rising fast; 3 = this week; 1 = no real deadline. (WSJF Time Criticality) |
| **Effort** (`E`) | 1–5 | Estimated hours bucketed: 1 = <1h, 2 = ~½ day, 3 = ~1 day, 4 = ~2-3 days, 5 = week+. (WSJF Job Size) |
| **Energy match** (`Gm`) | 0.8–1.2 | Multiplier: does this fit available energy *now*? 1.2 = perfect peak fit, 1.0 = neutral, 0.8 = needs more than you have. |
| **Project / company** (`P`) | tag | Not scored — used to (a) enforce day-theming and (b) ensure both companies get queue representation, preventing one from being starved. |
| **Avoidance flag** (`A`) | 0 / +1.0 boost | **The anti-freeze term.** Set true if the task has rolled over ≥3 days OR you notice dread. A flagged task gets a **priority boost**, not a penalty — because for this user, the avoided task is usually the important one hiding from the easy builds. |

**The formula:**

```
Base       = (Importance × 2 + Deadline) / Effort        ← WSJF spine; importance double-weighted
Score      = Base × EnergyMatch × (1 + AvoidanceBoost)   ← energy modulates, avoidance overrides
```

- **Importance is double-weighted** so a stressed brain can't let mere urgency (other people's Q3 fires) dominate the genuinely strategic work — the exact correction the Eisenhower critique demands.
- **÷ Effort** rewards quick high-value wins, deliberately surfacing momentum-builders — the single most effective way to break a freeze is a fast, real completion.
- **EnergyMatch** as a 0.8–1.2 multiplier nudges (never dictates) the queue toward what you can actually start right now.
- **AvoidanceBoost is the keystone.** Standard scoring models would let a scary, no-hard-deadline strategic task sink down the list — which is *exactly how the freeze wins*. Flagging rollover/dread and boosting it forces the avoided important work back to the top.

**Worked example.** "Company A board memo" — Importance 5, Deadline 3 (due Friday), Effort 3 (~1 day), rolled over 4 days (Avoidance flagged), and it's morning (EnergyMatch 1.2):
`Base = (5×2 + 3)/3 = 4.33` → `× 1.2 × (1 + 1.0) = 10.4`.
Versus "redesign the internal dashboard" (the appealing easy build) — Importance 2, Deadline 1, Effort 2, no avoidance, EnergyMatch 1.0:
`Base = (2×2 + 1)/2 = 2.5` → `× 1.0 × 1.0 = 2.5`.
The model puts the avoided board memo at the top (10.4 vs 2.5) — and the WIP cap of 3 means the dashboard build can't even be pulled until a higher-scored card is done. **The system makes the freeze's preferred move structurally impossible.**

**The output → daily ritual.** Each evening (shutdown): take the top of the weekly-scored queue, pick the **top 1–3** that fit tomorrow's WIP slots and day-theme, order them frog-first, and protect a morning block for #1. Each Friday (weekly review): re-score, re-flag avoidance, confirm the WIP cap held, and re-check the portfolio against the Buffett 5. Quarterly: re-cut the 5/25.

This gives the systems-thinker exactly what he'll trust — an inspectable, tunable model with explicit cost-of-delay math — while the WIP cap and the tiny ordered shortlist give the *freezer* what he needs: at any moment, exactly one obvious, permitted thing to do next.
